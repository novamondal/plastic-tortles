Stage 1 of Ocean Pollution Reduction: Recycling Reform

Problems:

Inability to recycle most plastics

Our current recycling systems mix together plastics, making it difficult to sort out and create usable recycled plastic products

Economically Impractical

Recycled plastic is generally more expensive and lower quality than new plastic, creating little incentive for companies to use recycled plastic and encouraging them to manufacture new plastic, contributing to pollution

Outsourcing

The biggest problem with our recycling programs is that the majority of our trash gets sold to other countries, where we have no control of the disposal of the waste. Most of these countries are poor or developing, and do not have the resources to properly process our plastic waste, leading them to improperly dispose of it. The net result is that the plastic ends up in the ocean

Solutions:

Nationalized Recycling Program and No Outsourcing of Recycling

By nationalizing our recycling program and keeping all of our recycled plastics domestic, we can ensure that all of our waste is properly processed and recycled. This will also create jobs in the recycling industry, and reduce the amount of plastic that ends up in the ocean. By nationalizing the industry, the government can also limit the economic obstructions to recycling.

Limiting Scope of Recycling Programs

Currently, our recycling programs generally collect plastic that cannot even be practically recycled. This creates a false public impression that plastic waste is less of an issue because it can be recycled, when in reality the majority gets landfilled. By making it clear that only certain types of plastic can be recycled, the public is more aware of where their trash actually ends up, leading to better public habits regarding plastic usage.

Limiting the scope of recycling programs to only a few types of plastic also makes it easier to sort through different types of plastic at recycling facilities, improving the efficiency of the recycling process.

Scope:

This would primarily involve the US, which produces the most plastic waste per capita. However, similar reforms could be put in place in any major developed country which produces a large amount of plastic waste.

Viability: Relatively High

There are no technological challenges to this stage of the plan, as the US already has the resources it needs to recycle its own plastic without exporting it (the primary reason recycled plastic currently gets exported is due to economic causes). The only barriers would be political, as the plastics and recycling industries would likely lobby against this kind of legislation. However, with enough public awareness and education, it should be relatively doable to pass recycling reform legislation.
